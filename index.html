1. Explain near-miss events. (18 marks)

Definition & difference: A near-miss is an occurrence that could have resulted in patient harm but was averted. Unlike adverse events (which cause harm), near-misses signal latent system flaws.

Examples: wrong medication selected but intercepted, intended imaging order not transmitted but detected, patient ID mismatch discovered before procedure.

Causes: human error, poor communication, unclear protocols, labeling/packaging issues, staffing/workload pressures, equipment failures.

Importance of reporting: Reporting near-misses uncovers system vulnerabilities without the consequence of actual harm; increases safety culture; prevents recurrence.

Investigation & response: Use non-punitive reporting; perform root-cause analysis (RCA); identify contributing factors; design corrective actions (process redesign, alerts, checklists).

Prevention strategies: standardise procedures, enforce double-checks (e.g., two-person drug checks), improve handovers, staff training, electronic barcoding/EMR safeguards, regular audits and feedback.

Outcome measures: reduced incidents, improved reporting rates, implemented corrective actions verified by follow-up audits.

3. Discuss importance of standardized protocol in healthcare to prevent sentinel events. (18 marks)

Definition & purpose: Standardized protocols are evidence-based, stepwise procedures (checklists, guidelines, order sets) that reduce variation in care and ensure critical safety steps are performed. Their purpose is to minimise human error and prevent catastrophic sentinel events.

Examples effective against sentinel events: WHO surgical safety checklist (pre-op verification, time-out), medication reconciliation at admission/discharge, barcoding for meds, central line insertion bundle, handoff/ISBAR communication protocol.

How protocols prevent sentinel events:

  • Reduce variability so routine tasks include safety checks (prevents wrong-site/wrong-patient procedures).
  • Force critical verifications (ID checks, consent, allergy status).
  • Standardise communication during handovers to avoid information loss.

Implementation strategies: leadership commitment, multidisciplinary development, staff training and simulation, integrate into workflow (EMR order sets), visual prompts, appoint champions, continuous monitoring.

Monitoring & improvement: track compliance, audit outcomes, perform root-cause analyses for breaches, use Plan-Do-Study-Act cycles to refine protocols.

Barriers & solutions: resistance to change (engage staff), workflow mismatch (pilot test), incomplete adherence (feedback, accountability).

Impact evidence: standard protocols consistently reduce surgical errors, medication errors, central line infections, and deaths â€” and create a culture of safety.

  1. Autonomy â€” respect patientâ€™s right to make informed decisions; requires informed consent and disclosure of options/risks.
  2. Beneficence â€” act to benefit the patient; balance potential benefits of interventions.
  3. Non-maleficence â€” avoid causing harm; weigh risks vs benefits and minimise iatrogenic harm.
  4. Justice â€” fair allocation of resources and impartial treatment.

Supporting ethical duties: Veracity (truthfulness), Confidentiality (protect patient information), Fidelity (keeping commitments), Duty of care.

Application in physiotherapy/BPT context:

  • Informed consent before treatment plans (explain goals, techniques, alternatives).
  • Autonomy: respect refusal of painful procedures; offer alternatives.
  • Beneficence & non-maleficence: choose evidence-based modalities to improve function while avoiding harmful over-treatment (e.g., avoid excessive aggressive mobilization causing injury).
  • Justice: equitable access to rehab services.

Decision framework for conflicts: identify stakeholders, list ethical principles in tension, gather facts, consider legal obligations, consult colleagues/ethics committee, choose least harmful option with documented justification.

Example scenario: A patient refuses a painful but potentially beneficial exercise â€” respect autonomy, re-explain benefits/risks, seek compromise (modify intensity), document discussion.
